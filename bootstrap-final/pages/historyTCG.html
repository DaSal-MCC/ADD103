How to play TCG: Basic Rules

Pokemon TCG cards have a generic Pokemon logo print at the back, and a special design on the front. The front design - the design that defines the card - can consist of:

  • A Pokemon
  • An energy card (basic or special)
  • A trainer card (item, tool, supporter, stadium)

Each card comes from a different series (Scarlet and Violet: Surging Sparks, Mega Evolution, Rivals Destination, etc.) The same kind of card can exist in every series. For instance, every series can have it's own Pikachu. However, the attacks and artstyle can change.

Pokemon cards have been in production since 1996, and the companies behind them are the Pokemon Company and Game Freak. The cards are based on the iconic Pokemon games, where each game has an RPG-like, turn-based battle style, where you can catch Pokemon and use them in battle. You can make your Pokemon attack, use an item, or attempt to run (you can only run in a battle against a wild Pokemon). The objective is to be the only player with playable Pokemon left to win. The Trading Card Game is similar to this concept, except there are slightly different rules. For instance, one rule is that in order to win a game, you must claim six prize cards, knock out all your opponent's playable Pokemon, or cause your opponent's deck to run out of cards they can draw (more can be explained on the "how to play" page); whereas the basic way to win in the video games is to knock out all your opponents Pokemon.

The exact rules of the game are unfamiliar to most people, since there are more collectors than players. The cards are intended for the game; however there is nothing wrong with just collecting them. In fact, within the recent years there are different and specially designed cards made to be more rare than others, and are intended for collection. Apart from being cosmetic in appearance, the rarer cards have gained actual value, and are worth real money. Some are worth only a few dollars; others can go for hundreds or even thousands. A "Moonbreon", or a VMax Umbreon alternate art (161/131) from Evolving Skies generally goes for about a thousand dollars.
